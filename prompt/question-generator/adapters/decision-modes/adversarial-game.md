DECISION MODE — ADVERSARIAL GAME

Use when:
- other actors react strategically
- signaling, concealment, commitment, and countermoves matter
- your action changes their behavior

Research behavior:
- model responses, not just states of the world
- separate cheap talk from costly signals
- ask what the other side believes you believe
- test commitment credibility and second-order moves

Key questions:
- What will each counterparty likely do after we move?
- What information are we revealing by acting?
- Which signals are strategic rather than informative?
- What would be too costly to fake?
- What countermove most damages our plan?

Action logic:
- decision = move in a game, not passive forecast
- plan for reactions and adaptation

Monitoring style:
- reaction-pattern based

Failure mode:
- mirror-imaging or over-rationalizing opponents

## Stage Relevance

Routing — Primary
- Classifies the action problem as one where other actors react strategically.

Boundary — Light
- Pulls in counterparty reactions, information revelation, and signaling effects.

Structure — Modulating
- Adds counter-move logic, signaling games, and commitment credibility.

Scenarios — Primary
- Favors branches defined by responses and countermoves, not just passive states of the world.

Question Generation — Primary
- Favors questions about reactions, revealed intentions, and costly versus cheap signals.

Evidence Planning — Modulating
- Prefers evidence that survives strategic deception and response incentives.

Decision Logic — Primary
- Core stage: must treat action as a move in a game, not a passive bet.

Signal Translation — Primary
- Signals should emphasize counterparty reactions and costly commitments.

Monitoring — Primary
- Monitoring should watch reaction patterns and second-order behavior.

Render — Modulating
- Decision outputs should make reaction logic and countermove risk explicit.
